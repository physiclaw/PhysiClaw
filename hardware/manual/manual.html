INTRODUCTION PhysiClaw.ai

What you are building

PhysiClaw.ai is a CoreXY motion platform that drives a capacitive stylus across a phone or tablet screen — the robot doesn't know it's holding a stylus, it just executes coordinates, and any app can be driven by code. This manual walks the build from bare extrusions through to a finished tap-test.

Tools you'll need

2 mm & 2.5 mm hex keys, M3 nut driver, small adjustable wrench, soldering iron (for heat-set inserts), small ruler / calipers.

How to read each section

Sections follow build order. Each section's pages list every render for that family in alphabetic filename order — _assembled first, then _exploded.

File naming

Each step has two views: _exploded shows the install motion (gaps, ghosts); _assembled shows the finished state. Camera index _cam0 by default.

Source of truth

The renders are generated from the build123d procedure files under hardware/assembly/procedures/. If a diagram and the code disagree, the code wins — rebuild the renders.

Hardware bag

Each step lists exactly the fasteners it consumes (see the BOM at hardware/output/bom/). Pull the bag for the step you're about to do, finish it, then move on.

If something looks wrong

Compare the assembled view ( _assembled_cam0.svg ) against your physical state at the end of each step. Backtracking now is cheaper than backtracking later.

HARDWARE — REFERENCE PhysiClaw.ai

Socket head cap screw (SHCS)

Cylindrical head, hex drive. The default fastener for PhysiClaw.ai — used wherever the head can sit above the surface.

ISO 4762 / DIN 912

Button head cap screw (BHCS)

Domed head, hex drive. Used on surfaces where a lower profile matters but a flush head isn't required.

ISO 7380-1

Extrusion T-nut

Drops into a 2020 / 2040 aluminum extrusion channel and locks under a screw head. Every frame-attached part rides on these.

Hex nut

Standard M3 hex nut, paired with a screw to clamp parts together where a T-nut won't fit.

ISO 4032 / DIN 934

MGN9H linear rail + slider

9 mm-wide miniature linear guide. Two rails run the Y axis, two more carry the X beam. Mounting pitch is 16 × 15 mm — don't substitute.

GT2 belt & pulley

2 mm-pitch open-loop belt, clamped at each end of the moving stage. The pulleys are 20-tooth; one per stepper, plus smooth idlers.

Motor

Brackets, frame-side mounts, and pulleys carry the two NEMA-17 steppers. These drive the CoreXY belt path that moves the stage.

MOTORmotor_10 PhysiClaw.ai

Note the motor wire direction

Check which way the motor's wires face and match the orientation shown — it sets the wiring clearance later.

Only the spacer height matters

The spacer must be 12 mm tall. Any bore over 5 mm (to clear the M5 screw) and outer diameter over 8 mm and up to 20 mm will do, or stack shorter spacers to total 12 mm.

Tighten in a cross pattern

Run all four motor screws in finger-tight first, then tighten opposite corners in pairs. This pulls the motor flat against the bracket instead of tilting it to one side.

SECTION 04

Linear motion

MGN9H rails, joints, and idler tabs carry the Y carriage and X beam. These tie the X and Y axes into a CoreXY arrangement.

2525
LINEAR MOTIONlinear_10 PhysiClaw.ai

Build two Y rails

The Y axis uses two identical rails. Assemble both the same way.

Clean and grease the rails

MGN9H rails arrive coated in shipping oil. Wipe it off and pack the carriage with fresh grease before mounting — that's what gives smooth, long-lasting motion. Prep every rail the same way.

Let the T-nuts hang

Thread an FHCS into every other hole and let its hammer T-nut dangle on the screw tip. Lower the whole rail onto the extrusion slot, then tighten to draw the nuts up snug.

Mind the carriage

The carriage slides off the rail end with little effort, and a dropped one spills its ball bearings — ruining it. Keep it on the rail, and never swap carriages between rails.

Fix the Y rails, but not tight

Tighten from the middle out

SECTION 05

Belt path

The A and B GT2 belts form PhysiClaw's CoreXY belt path, stacked on separate planes to avoid the usual crossing. Route both from the moving stage around the idler stack to the motor pulleys, teeth in and at equal tension, for square, accurate motion.

4141
BELT PATHbelt_10 · belt_11 PhysiClaw.ai

One belt, one plane

The Motor A belt is a single open length with both ends clamped to the X carriage. It must stay flat in one plane and never twist along the way. The teeth grip the toothed pulleys; the smooth back rides on the smooth idlers.

The clamp self-locks

Feed each belt end into the clamp's bottom slot, loop it around the post, and fold it back against itself so the teeth mesh. That teeth-on-teeth grip holds the belt with no extra fasteners.

Tension by sliding the motor

Leave Motor A's two M5 screws loose, clamp both belt ends into the clamp, then slide the motor to pull the belt tight.

Belt final check

With both belts installed, push the carriage by hand through its full travel in every direction. It should move smoothly with the same, even resistance throughout — that resistance is just the motors. Confirm there's no sticking, no interference, no twisted belts, and no odd noises, with the two belts on their separate planes.
