INTRODUCTION PhysiClaw.ai

Reporting an issue

Should you find an error in this manual or have a suggestion for an improvement, please consider opening an issue on GitHub (github.com/echosprint/PhysiClaw/issues). When raising an issue please include the relevant page numbers and a short description; annotated screenshots are also very welcome. We periodically update the manual based on the feedback we get.

This is just a reference

This manual is designed to be a simple build reference. Assembling a PhysiClaw can be an involved endeavour, so for any section you need clarification on we recommend opening the .step file in FreeCAD — it is often easier to follow along with the whole assembly in front of you.

HARDWARE — REFERENCE PhysiClaw.ai

Socket head cap screw (SHCS)

Cylindrical head, hex drive. The default fastener for PhysiClaw.ai — used wherever the head can sit above the surface.

ISO 4762 / DIN 912

Button head cap screw (BHCS)

Domed head, hex drive. Used on surfaces where a lower profile matters but a flush head isn't required.

ISO 7380-1

Extrusion T-nut

Drops into a 2020 / 2040 aluminum extrusion channel and locks under a screw head. Every frame-attached part rides on these.

Hex nut

Standard M3 hex nut, paired with a screw to clamp parts together where a T-nut won't fit.

ISO 4032 / DIN 934

MGN9H linear rail + slider

9 mm-wide miniature linear guide. Two rails run the Y axis, two more carry the X beam. Mounting pitch is 16 × 15 mm — don't substitute.

GT2 belt & pulley

2 mm-pitch open-loop belt, clamped at each end of the moving stage. The pulleys are 20-tooth; one per stepper, plus smooth idlers.

3D PRINTED PARTS PhysiClaw.ai

Part printing settings and guidelines

PhysiClaw's custom parts are printed by Selective Laser Sintering (SLS), not FDM. A laser fuses nylon powder in a heated powder bed, so parts come out fully solid, support-free, and strong in every direction. This matters here: the parts carry side holes and overhangs that FDM cannot print cleanly. Match the specifications below when sourcing parts.

3D Printing Process

Selective Laser Sintering (SLS)

Part Density

Solid (no infill)

Material

Black PA12 nylon (or equivalent)

Dimensional Tolerance

±0.3% (min ±0.3 mm)

Layer Thickness

100 µm (0.1 mm)

Orientation

Service default — no supports

Minimum Wall Thickness

1.0 mm

Surface Finish

Raw / as-printed

File naming

Any file whose name ends in _x# tells you the quantity of that part required to build the machine — for example a part named idler_mount_front_x2.step is needed two times.

SECTION 04

Linear motion

MGN9H rails, joints, and idler tabs carry the Y carriage and X beam. These tie the X and Y axes into a CoreXY arrangement.

2626
LINEAR MOTIONlinear_10 PhysiClaw.ai

Build two Y rails

The Y axis uses two identical rails. Assemble both the same way.

Clean and grease the rails

MGN9H rails arrive coated in shipping oil. Wipe it off and pack the carriage with fresh grease before mounting — that's what gives smooth, long-lasting motion. Prep every rail the same way.

Let the T-nuts hang

Thread an FHCS into every other hole and let its hammer T-nut dangle on the screw tip. Lower the whole rail onto the extrusion slot, then tighten to draw the nuts up snug.

Mind the carriage

The carriage slides off the rail end with little effort, and a dropped one spills its ball bearings — ruining it. Keep it on the rail, and never swap carriages between rails.

SECTION 05

Belt path

The A and B GT2 belts form PhysiClaw's CoreXY belt path, stacked on separate planes to avoid the usual crossing. Route both from the moving stage around the idler stack to the motor pulleys, teeth in and at equal tension, for square, accurate motion.

4242
BELT PATHbelt_10 · belt_11 PhysiClaw.ai

One belt, one plane

The Motor A belt is a single open length with both ends clamped to the X carriage. It must stay flat in one plane and never twist along the way. The teeth grip the toothed pulleys; the smooth back rides on the smooth idlers.

The clamp self-locks

Feed each belt end into the clamp's bottom slot, loop it around the post, and fold it back against itself so the teeth mesh. That teeth-on-teeth grip holds the belt with no extra fasteners.

Tension by sliding the motor

Leave Motor A's two M5 screws loose, clamp both belt ends into the clamp, then slide the motor to pull the belt tight.

Belt final check

With both belts installed, push the carriage by hand through its full travel in every direction. It should move smoothly with the same, even resistance throughout — that resistance is just the motors. Confirm there's no sticking, no interference, no twisted belts, and no odd noises, with the two belts on their separate planes.

4545
BELT PATHbelt_30 PhysiClaw.ai
75Hz

Belt tension

Equal tension on both belts is essential for a CoreXY motion system. Even a slight difference between the two will skew the gantry's motion. You can adjust a motor's position to fine-tune the tension of its belt.

Pluck the belts

For a quick check, move the X carriage fully to the motor side and pluck the indicated belts with a finger. The two should feel and sound about the same — matching pitch means roughly equal tension.

75Hz AT 220mm

For a precise check, measure each belt with a tuning app on your phone while the gantry is parked at the marked position. Pluck the belts and aim for 75 Hz — both reading the same frequency confirms the tension is correct.

PHONE BEDphone_40 PhysiClaw.ai

Install the phone bed

Stand the frame on end first — it's far easier to work vertically. Then slide each beam's T-nuts into the slots on the frame's underside.

Position the phone

Place a phone on the bed, pushed into the top-left corner. Jog the X carriage across its full travel and check the stylus tip can reach every part of the screen.

Dial in the tap

The tip must tap firmly enough to register, but no harder. Too hard can crack the screen; too far or too light and the tap misses. Adjust the solenoid mount height until taps land reliably.

Keep the screen flat

The rear camera bump lifts one corner, so the phone won't lie flat. Slip a thin shim or a few sheets of paper under the low edge until the screen is level and facing straight up. Make sure the phone can't rock or shift when the stylus taps — any tilt or wobble can make it miss.
